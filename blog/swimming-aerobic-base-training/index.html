The Aerobic System Is Your Engine

Swimming is 70-80% aerobic at distances of 200m and above. Yet most recreational swimmers spend the majority of their training time at threshold or above — the exact opposite of what coaches recommend. The aerobic base provides the physiological infrastructure: more mitochondria, greater capillary density, improved fat oxidation. Without it, interval training produces diminishing returns.

Zone 2 Swimming: What It Looks Like

Zone 2 for swimming is roughly 60-75% of max heart rate — conversational pace, sustainable for 30-60 minutes. Most recreational swimmers find this pace feels embarrassingly slow. That's the point. Research from Dr. Iñigo San Millán at the University of Colorado shows that elite endurance athletes spend 75-80% of training volume in Zone 2. The adaptations take 8-12 weeks to manifest fully.

The 80/20 Rule in the Pool

Polarized training — 80% easy, 20% hard — has been validated across multiple endurance sports. A 2013 meta-analysis in the International Journal of Sports Physiology confirmed superior adaptations in polarized vs. threshold-heavy training approaches. Apply this to your swim week: if you swim 4 days, 3 should be at easy aerobic pace, 1 should include genuine high-intensity work.

Building Your Base: A 6-Week Framework

Weeks 1-2: All aerobic, no intervals. Focus on time in the water, not pace. Weeks 3-4: Introduce technique sets (drills count as rest). Weeks 5-6: Add one interval session per week, keeping 80% of volume easy. Resist the urge to push every session. Fatigue accumulation without adequate aerobic base leads to stagnation and injury.
