The Dirty Truth About the Kick

The goal is not a powerful kick; it's an efficient, drag-reducing kick that preserves oxygen for your pull.

Two-Beat vs. Six-Beat: Which Is Right for You

Elite distance swimmers typically use a 2-beat kick (one kick per arm pull). Elite sprinters use 6-beat. Triathletes often use 2-beat or 0-beat. Research supports matching kick pattern to event demands: 2-beat conserves energy and reduces oxygen cost in events above 400m. 6-beat generates more body rotation and power for sprints. Forcing a 6-beat kick in a 1500m is a common mistake that leads to leg fatigue by the final 400m.

The Biomechanics of an Efficient Kick

A correct flutter kick originates from the hip, not the knee. The knee should have slight flex, with the down-kick driving from hip flexion and the up-kick from hip extension — like a whip motion through the leg. Feet should be slightly turned inward (pigeon-toed) with relaxed ankles. A rigid, bicycle-pedal kick bends the knee too much and creates drag rather than propulsion.

Ankle Flexibility: The Often-Missed Limiter

Ankle plantar flexion range is strongly correlated with kick effectiveness. A 2010 study in the Journal of Sports Sciences found that elite swimmers had significantly greater ankle flexibility than recreational swimmers, allowing a larger propulsive arc. Work ankle stretches daily: seated forward lean with foot extended, toe-pointed stretches, and resistance band plantar flexion holds. Even modest improvement in ROM improves kick propulsion.
