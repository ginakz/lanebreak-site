What Taper Actually Does Physiologically

Taper is not just 'resting before a race.' It's a specific physiological process. Research from the University of Otago shows that a proper taper increases red blood cell mass, improves neuromuscular recruitment patterns, restores glycogen stores to super-compensated levels, and allows microtrauma in muscle fibers to repair. These adaptations peak at approximately 7-14 days post-load reduction.

The Volume Mistake: Cutting Too Much

The most common taper error is dropping volume too aggressively. A 2011 meta-analysis in Sports Medicine found that volume reductions of 41-60% produce better performance gains than reductions above 60%. Cutting more than 60% of training volume too quickly leads to detraining rather than sharpening. The sweet spot: reduce volume by 40-50%, maintain or slightly increase intensity.

Maintain Intensity During Taper

Counter-intuitively, you should not back off from high-intensity work during taper. Race-pace sets and above should continue — just shorter. A 10-day taper might look like: days 1-3 normal intensity, reduced volume; days 4-6 lighter volume with 2-3 short sprint sets; days 7-9 very light, but include 4-6 race-pace 50s; day 10 rest or very easy 20 minutes.

Taper Length by Event Distance

The appropriate taper length scales with event distance and training volume. Sprint events (50-100m): 5-7 day taper. Middle distance (200-400m): 10-14 days. Distance (800m+): 14-21 days. Heavy training loads require longer tapers because more repair is needed. Don't use a sprint taper before a distance event — you'll arrive under-prepared physiologically.
